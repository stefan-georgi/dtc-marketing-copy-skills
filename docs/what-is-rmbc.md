# What Is RMBC?

RMBC is a structured framework for writing direct response (DR) copy that sells. It stands for **Research, Mechanism, Brief, Copy** — four phases that turn a blank page into high-converting sales copy.

Created by Stefan Georgi, one of the most successful direct response copywriters alive (with $700M+ in attributed client results), RMBC exists because most copywriting advice boils down to "write better." That is not a system. RMBC is.

## The Problem RMBC Solves

Most copywriters start writing before they understand what they are selling, who they are selling to, or why anyone should believe the product works. The result is generic copy that sounds like everything else in the market.

RMBC forces you to do the hard thinking before you write a single headline. Each phase builds on the last. Skip a phase and the copy breaks.

## The Four Phases

### 1. Research

Before writing anything, you study three things:
- **The product** — what it does, how it works, what makes it different
- **The prospect** — who buys this, what they have tried before, what they believe, what they fear
- **The market** — what competitors say, what claims are already saturated, where the gaps are

Good research means you can describe the prospect's problem better than they can. That is what earns attention.

### 2. Mechanism

This is the phase most frameworks skip entirely, and it is the most important one.

A mechanism answers the question: **"Why does this product work?"** Not what it does — why it works. The mechanism is the unique process, ingredient, technology, or method that makes the product's promise believable.

Examples:
- A weight loss supplement does not just "burn fat." It contains a high-altitude plant compound that activates brown adipose tissue. That compound is the mechanism.
- A productivity app does not just "save time." It uses a 3-step daily capture system based on cognitive load research. That system is the mechanism.

Without a mechanism, your copy makes a claim with no engine behind it. Prospects have heard every claim before. The mechanism is what makes them think, "Oh, that is different."

### 3. Brief

The brief is your blueprint. Before writing copy, you organize your research and mechanism into a structured outline:
- **The Big Idea** — the single compelling concept that ties everything together
- **The lead** — how you will open (problem, story, news, secret, etc.)
- **Key proof points** — testimonials, data, clinical studies, demonstrations
- **The offer structure** — what is included, bonuses, guarantee, price anchoring
- **Emotional arc** — the journey from problem to solution to action

The brief prevents copy from meandering. Every section has a job. Every paragraph earns the next.

### 4. Copy

Only now do you write. With research done, mechanism identified, and brief structured, the actual writing becomes the easiest part. You are not inventing — you are assembling.

The copy phase covers headlines, leads, body copy, transitions, proof sections, closes, and calls to action. Because the foundation is solid, the copy tends to be specific, believable, and differentiated.

## How RMBC Differs from Generic Frameworks

Most copywriting frameworks (AIDA, PAS, BAB) describe the **structure of the output** — attention, interest, desire, action. They tell you what sections to include but not how to fill them.

RMBC describes the **process of creation**. It is a workflow, not a template. The framework applies whether you are writing a 30-second Facebook ad or a 90-minute VSL (video sales letter). The phases scale; the templates do not.

The other key difference is the mechanism phase. No other widely-used copywriting framework treats mechanism identification as a distinct, mandatory step. This is what gives RMBC-trained copywriters a structural advantage — their copy has an engine that competing copy lacks.

## Why Mechanism Is the Key Differentiator

In saturated markets, every product makes similar claims. "Lose weight fast." "Grow your business." "Sleep better tonight." The prospect has heard all of it.

A strong mechanism cuts through because it gives the prospect a **new reason to believe**. It is not louder — it is different. When a prospect encounters a mechanism they have not seen before, they re-engage with a category they had tuned out.

Stefan's insight was that mechanism is not a nice-to-have flourish. It is the structural element that determines whether copy converts or gets ignored.
