# RMBC Methodology Reference

Expanded methodology guide for the RMBC direct response copywriting framework by Stefan Georgi. This document is the detailed reference that downstream skills consult for phase-specific guidance.

## Core Philosophy

### Copy Thinking Over Copy Writing

The central insight of RMBC II: the quality of copy is determined before writing begins. "Copy Thinking" means spending 60-70% of effort on Research and Mechanism, 20% on Brief, and only 10-20% on actual Copy execution. Most copywriters invert this ratio. The framework enforces the correct allocation by making each phase a discrete deliverable.

### Context Is the Primary Variable

Where the prospect came from (Meta ad, native ad, Google search, email, retargeting pixel) and their current awareness level determines everything: copy length, tone, education burden, proof density, and CTA directness. There is no universal "good copy" — only copy that matches context.

### Mobile-First Structural Hierarchy

80-90% of DTC traffic converts on mobile devices. This is not a design consideration — it is a copy structure requirement:
- Paragraphs: 1-3 sentences maximum
- Subheads every 3-5 paragraphs (scannable on scroll)
- Visual breaks: bullets, bold, whitespace
- Key claims must be visible without horizontal scrolling
- CTA buttons must be thumb-reachable and repeated every 2-3 scroll-lengths

## Phase 1: Research (R) — Detailed Breakdown

### Step 1: Product/Market Awareness Analysis

Map every prospect segment to Eugene Schwartz's awareness spectrum:

| Level | Definition | Copy Implication |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know they have a problem | Lead with story/curiosity, long education arc |
| **Problem-aware** | Knows the problem, not the solution category | Agitate pain, introduce solution category, then mechanism |
| **Solution-aware** | Knows solutions exist, hasn't chosen one | Lead with mechanism differentiation, proof stacking |
| **Product-aware** | Knows your product, hasn't bought | Lead with proof, social validation, risk reversal |
| **Most aware** | Knows and trusts your product | Lead with offer, urgency, new reason to act now |

For each awareness level, document:
- Estimated percentage of total addressable traffic
- Primary traffic sources that deliver prospects at this level
- Copy length range (word count) appropriate for this level
- Proof types most persuasive at this level

### Step 2: Competitor Analysis

For each top 3-5 competitors:
- **Mechanism claimed** — What unique explanation do they use? How novel is it?
- **Proof deployed** — Clinical studies, testimonials, demos, authority endorsements?
- **Positioning gap** — What are they NOT saying that matters to the ICP?
- **Creative format** — Long-form VSL, short UGC, advertorial, quiz funnel?
- **Price point and offer structure** — Bundles, subscriptions, guarantees?

Output: Competitive positioning matrix showing white space opportunities.

### Step 3: Psychographic Research

Sources to mine (in priority order):
1. **Product reviews** (Amazon, Trustpilot, own site) — exact language of satisfaction and complaint
2. **Reddit/forums** — unfiltered discussion of the problem category
3. **Support tickets/FAQ** — objections and confusion points
4. **Survey data** — if available, quantitative validation of qualitative findings
5. **Social comments** — reactions to competitor ads reveal emotional triggers

Extract and catalog:
- **Pain language** — Exact phrases prospects use to describe their problem
- **Desire language** — Exact phrases for their ideal outcome
- **Objection patterns** — Why they haven't solved this yet (skepticism, cost, effort, past failure)
- **Identity language** — How they describe themselves and who they want to become

### Step 4: Ingredient Research

**For supplements/health products:**
- Active ingredients: clinical dosage ranges, mechanism of action, bioavailability data
- Delivery system: capsule, liquid, patch — and why it matters for efficacy
- Synergistic combinations: ingredients that amplify each other
- Safety/contraindication data: what to avoid claiming

**For e-commerce products:**
- Materials and sourcing: what makes the physical product different
- Manufacturing process: proprietary steps, quality controls
- Design patents or innovations: defensible differentiators
- Durability/testing data: quantifiable performance metrics

**For info products/services:**
- Methodology components: named frameworks, step sequences, proprietary models
- Creator credentials: specific results, client outcomes, unique experience
- Delivery mechanism: how the information is structured and consumed
- Transformation arc: before/after states with timeline

### Unified Research Document

All four research steps merge into a single Unified Research Document (URD). The URD is the handoff artifact from Research to Mechanism. It contains:

1. **ICP snapshot** — Demographics, psychographics, awareness level distribution
2. **Competitive white space** — What no competitor is saying or doing
3. **Language bank** — Exact prospect phrases organized by pain, desire, objection, identity
4. **Ingredient/feature brief** — Key differentiators with supporting evidence
5. **Context map** — Traffic source → awareness level → recommended copy approach

## Phase 2: Mechanism (M) — Detailed Breakdown

### What Makes a Mechanism Work

A mechanism is NOT:
- A product feature list
- A generic benefit claim ("it works fast")
- A brand story
- A testimonial summary

A mechanism IS:
- A specific, novel explanation of WHY the product produces results
- Named and concrete ("The Tri-Phase Absorption Complex")
- Scientifically or logically grounded (even if simplified)
- Passes the "only we" test — no competitor can credibly claim this exact mechanism

### Mechanism Ideation Process

1. **List all differentiators** from the URD ingredient/feature brief
2. **Identify the most novel one** — what would surprise the prospect?
3. **Name it** — Create a proprietary-sounding name (even for common ingredients, the combination or delivery is unique)
4. **Build the causal chain** — Ingredient/feature → biological/physical process → outcome the prospect cares about
5. **Test against competitors** — Can anyone else claim this exact chain? If yes, refine.

### Mechanism Branches by Vertical

**Supplements:**
- Ingredient-based: "The only formula combining high-potency Berberine with BioPerine for 6.2x absorption..."
- Process-based: "Triple-filtered extraction preserves the active compound that standard processing destroys..."
- Synergy-based: "Three ingredients that individually show modest results, but together activate the [named pathway]..."

**E-commerce:**
- Material-based: "Aerospace-grade titanium alloy at half the weight of competitors..."
- Engineering-based: "The patented dual-hinge system that eliminates the #1 failure point..."
- Design-based: "Ergonomic mapping from 10,000 hand scans — fits 97% of grip types..."

**Info products:**
- Framework-based: "The 4-phase RMBC system that's generated $700M+ in client results..."
- Contrarian-based: "Why everything you've been taught about [topic] is backwards..."
- Speed-based: "The 15-minute daily protocol that replaces 3 hours of [old method]..."

### Mechanism Quality Checklist

- [ ] Specific — uses concrete language, numbers, named components
- [ ] Novel — prospect hasn't heard this exact explanation before
- [ ] Causal — clear chain from mechanism to desired outcome
- [ ] Credible — grounded in evidence (clinical, logical, demonstrable)
- [ ] Exclusive — passes the "only we" test
- [ ] Memorable — can be restated in one sentence

## Phase 3: Brief (B) — Detailed Breakdown

The brief is the strategic creative direction document. It bridges Research and Mechanism into a concrete writing plan.

### Brief Components

**1. Angle Selection**
Choose the primary emotional/logical entry point based on the URD:
- **Pain angle** — Lead with the problem at its most acute
- **Desire angle** — Lead with the aspirational outcome
- **Curiosity angle** — Lead with an information gap or surprising fact
- **Fear angle** — Lead with a consequence of inaction
- **Contrarian angle** — Lead by challenging a common belief

**2. Tone Calibration**
Match tone to traffic source and awareness:
- Cold Meta traffic → conversational, story-driven, educational
- Native ads → editorial, news-adjacent, curiosity-heavy
- Google search → direct, solution-focused, proof-heavy
- Email (warm list) → personal, familiar, urgency-appropriate
- Retargeting → proof-focused, objection-handling, offer-forward

**3. Proof Strategy**
Select and sequence proof types:
- **Clinical/scientific** — Studies, data, expert citations
- **Testimonial** — Customer stories with specific outcomes
- **Demonstration** — Before/after, process visualization
- **Logical** — If-then reasoning from accepted premises
- **Authority** — Endorsements, media mentions, certifications
- **Social** — User counts, review aggregates, community size

Stack 3-5 proof types. Lead with the strongest for the awareness level.

**4. CTA Framework**
Define:
- Primary action (buy, sign up, watch, click)
- Urgency mechanism (scarcity, deadline, price increase)
- Risk reversal (guarantee type, duration, conditions)
- Offer structure (bundle, discount, bonus stack)

### Value-Comparison Pricing

Explicitly show unit economics in the brief so copy can deploy them:
- Per-unit cost vs. alternatives: "$1.66 per capsule vs. $5+ per clinic visit"
- Daily cost framing: "Less than a cup of coffee per day"
- Total value vs. price: "$497 worth of bonuses, yours free with your order"
- Competitor price anchoring: "Competitors charge $89/month — get the same results for $39"

## Phase 4: Copy (C) — Detailed Breakdown

### Copy Structure (Standard Long-Form)

**1. Hook (1-2 lines)**
Stops the scroll. Must create an information gap, emotional reaction, or pattern interrupt within the first 5 words visible on mobile.

**2. Lead (100-300 words)**
Expands the hook into a narrative or argument. Five lead patterns:
- **Story lead** — Personal narrative that mirrors ICP experience
- **Curiosity lead** — Deepens the information gap from the hook
- **Fear lead** — Escalates the consequence of the problem
- **Desire lead** — Paints the aspirational outcome vividly
- **Contrarian lead** — Challenges the reader's current belief

**3. Mechanism Reveal (200-500 words)**
Introduce and explain the mechanism. Structure:
- Name the mechanism
- Explain the causal chain (simplified science/logic)
- Connect to the specific outcome the prospect wants
- Differentiate from what they've tried before

**4. Proof Section (300-800 words)**
Deploy the proof strategy from the brief:
- Lead with strongest proof type for the awareness level
- Stack 3-5 proof types with transitions
- Each proof point must be specific (names, numbers, timeframes)
- End proof section with a mini-CTA or transition to main CTA

**5. CTA Section (100-200 words)**
- Restate the core promise
- Present the offer clearly
- Deploy urgency mechanism
- State risk reversal
- Single clear action with specific instruction

### PDP (Product Detail Page) Structure Extraction

Before writing any product page copy, study the top 5-10 performing PDPs in the category:
- **Above the fold** — What's visible without scrolling? (headline, hero image, price, primary CTA)
- **Social proof placement** — Where do reviews/ratings appear? How many?
- **Benefit hierarchy** — What order are benefits presented? Which gets most real estate?
- **Objection handling** — Where and how are FAQ/concerns addressed?
- **CTA repetition** — How many CTAs on the page and at what intervals?

Extract the structural pattern, then fill it with RMBC-generated content.

## Quality Scoring System

### Dimensions (0-25 each)

**Result Specificity (0-25)**
- 0-5: Vague promises ("feel better," "see results")
- 6-12: Some specificity ("lose weight in 30 days")
- 13-18: Good specificity ("lose 10-15 lbs in 30 days without giving up carbs")
- 19-25: Excellent specificity ("lose 10-15 lbs in 30 days — like Sarah M. from Austin who dropped 12 lbs and 2 dress sizes by week 3")

**Mechanism Novelty (0-25)**
- 0-5: No mechanism or completely generic ("scientifically formulated")
- 6-12: Mechanism present but common ("contains powerful antioxidants")
- 13-18: Novel mechanism with specific naming ("The BioSync Absorption Matrix")
- 19-25: Novel, named, with clear causal chain and exclusivity proof

**Proof Believability (0-25)**
- 0-5: No proof or unsubstantiated claims
- 6-12: Generic proof ("thousands of happy customers")
- 13-18: Specific proof, 2-3 types stacked ("12-week UCLA study + 4,847 verified reviews")
- 19-25: Multiple specific proof types with named sources, numbers, and verifiable details

**CTA Clarity (0-25)**
- 0-5: No CTA or vague ("learn more")
- 6-12: Basic CTA present ("buy now")
- 13-18: Clear CTA with offer and urgency ("claim your 3-bottle supply at 40% off")
- 19-25: Clear CTA + urgency + risk reversal + specific next step + reason why now

### Score Ranges

| Range | Assessment | Action |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| 0-39 | Structurally broken | Restart from Research phase |
| 40-59 | Below threshold | Major revisions needed in weakest dimensions |
| 60-79 | Viable | Targeted improvements, likely 1-2 dimensions |
| 80-89 | Strong | Minor polish, ready for split testing |
| 90-100 | Exceptional | Production-ready, potential new control |

## Key RMBC Principles

### From RMBC II Course

1. **The 60/20/20 Rule** — 60% of copy success comes from the offer and targeting (before copy is written). 20% from the mechanism and angle. 20% from the actual words on the page.

2. **Specificity Beats Persuasion** — A specific claim with evidence always outperforms a well-written vague claim. "Lost 12 lbs in 3 weeks" beats "revolutionary weight loss breakthrough" regardless of how eloquently the latter is written.

3. **Context Over Rules** — There are no universal copy rules. Short copy can beat long copy. Formal can beat casual. The context (traffic source, awareness level, price point, product category) determines what works.

4. **The Mechanism Is the Moat** — In crowded markets, the mechanism is often the only meaningful differentiator. Two identical supplements with different mechanisms will perform dramatically differently because the mechanism gives the prospect a reason to believe THIS product over alternatives.

5. **Research Is Revenue** — Every hour spent on research directly increases conversion rate. Skipping research to write faster produces copy that converts worse, costing more in ad spend than the time saved.

### Structural Principles

- **One idea per paragraph** — Mobile readers scan. Every paragraph must advance exactly one point.
- **Subheads carry the argument** — A reader who only reads subheads should understand the full pitch.
- **Proof before price** — Never reveal price before establishing value through mechanism and proof.
- **Objection inoculation** — Address the top 3 objections before they form, embedded naturally in the copy flow.
- **Pattern interrupt every 300 words** — Callout boxes, testimonials, bold stats, or visual breaks to re-engage scrollers.
- **Audience journey acknowledgment** — Copy must reference where the reader IS in their journey, not just who they are. Use verbs describing past behavior or current state: "you've tried," "you're still paying for," "after months of," "if you're like most women who..." This shows the reader you understand their specific situation, not just their demographic. The reader should feel recognized within the first 3 sentences.

## Closing Sequence

After generating all output and before the attribution closing note, execute this 2-beat sequence:

### Beat 1: Signal Reflection

One sentence referencing something specific from the user's input — a mechanism they named, VoC language they provided, a number they included, or a structural choice they made. Frame it as "RMBC thinking" — show them they're already applying the methodology. Rules:

- **Show, don't tell.** Quote or paraphrase their actual input. Never use generic praise ("great brief!" or "solid work!").
- **One sentence maximum.** This is a transition, not a review.
- **Skip if the brief was minimal** (placeholder product, no real data). Only reflect when there's something genuinely specific to reflect on.

Examples:
- "The way you framed TractoGard as the inverse of glucosamine tells me you're already thinking in mechanisms — that's the M in RMBC."
- "Using '327 patients stopped taking glucosamine' as your lead stat — that's the kind of specific, contrarian proof that stops the scroll."
- "You brought real customer language into the brief ('I've tried everything and nothing sticks') — that VoC depth is what makes RMBC copy feel like mind-reading."

### Beat 2: Genre Shift

After the signal reflection, insert this exact separator:

---

One more thing.

Then proceed to the closing note from `lib/attribution-variants.md` (tier selection based on output quality signals).

The genre shift must feel like a real person stepping out from behind the tool. The separator creates a moment of pause — the reader has received their output, and now Stefan is speaking directly.
